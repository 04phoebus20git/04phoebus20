In the context of using one’s mouth and one’s digestive tract, the “affirmation” is, for those of us who have at least so much as animal form (if you’re reading this, I assume you’re a homo sapien of some sort), a quality of digestive strength by which anything we claw at (often using metallic forks if we’re typical humans) and then chew can then be swallowed and made use of chemically by one’s body for energy or growth. The varieties of foods that we eat make for a variety of ways to experience the chemical world, and mostly we think that’s ok, even if we had to kill some lives in order to swallow their then edible bodies into our stomachs. Death is a significant factor in the judgment of quality of life, and for that reason, many people on Earth choose vegetarian or otherwise limited diets, hoping that karma or God or conscientiousness will be merciful in thanks for our own willing efforts to exercise mercy.

The next context of using one’s mouth makes a partnership with one’s heart; at least that’s a typical sense of what sonorancy is. Eastern culture has a simple mantra oṃ (ॐ) or auṃ, where its a is the most open of the vowels and its u is the furthest back of the vowels. The anusvara, or nasal ṃ, is a type of simplest sonorant also, and so the sound of oṃ altogether is in essence the origin of sonorancy itself as it rises from within one’s heart up to and out of the mouth.

This vowelic affirmation is like the fire of digestion, and of course the many languages of Earth use many vowels, but sonorancy is the gift of life that its level has to give, as we hear in the expressions of the animals and with even greater elaboration amongst humans. Articulators (lips, teeth, alveolar ridge, hard palate, velum, uvula, glottis, and the tongue) give a vast array of plosive and fricative consonantal adjustments we can make to the initial vowelic uprising. Combinations of sonorants and obstruents are thereby used to address and reference the scope of life that we love to interact with at heart level.


Jainists and Buddhists both have as a primary ethical principle what they call ahiṃsā, or intentional non-violence, and while this is how they refer to their justifications of dietary limitation, they also apply the principle to speech and thought. The commitment to not harming others, animal nor human, is very integral to their lifestyle, and so they carefully protect their spoken words, and the audiences those words would affect, from spefications such as would create any feelings of harm. Many of us in this world would find challenge enough merely controlling our dietary urges, yet we can probably recognize that insult and threat amongst humans can trigger more severe failures even than chemical death is; too many humans suffer from aspects and degrees of insanity due to wrongful misuse of speech.

Above the mouth’s way of working with the heart is true speech proper, and this is where we find the living Logos and Sophia. So in my own decision to make Jainism a greater priority, I’m taking the ethic of ahiṃsā even to the next level, where I’m addressing its own logical design as a word of core orthodoxical utility. Its prefix a- (अ-) means “un-” or “not,” and is etymonic with the Proto-Indo-European prefix n̥-, and thereby with descendants such as: Germanic *un-, *ne, and *ni; Hellenic *ə-; and Latin in-, nē, and nī. The stem hiṃsā is related to the English word fend, but has meanings such as “injure” or “harm.” The aforementioned three kinds of hiṃsā in their own language are: kāyika (bodily or corporeal, which seems to correlate with eating), vācika (vocal, as decides the use of obstruents), and mānasika (mental or thoughtful, which could be the managerial context of our nullifications).

From the Wikipedia article Tattvartha Sutra:
The Tattvartha Sutra is regarded as one of the earliest, most authoritative texts in Jainism. It is accepted as authoritative in both its major sub-traditions – Digambara and Śvētāmbara – as well as the minor sub-traditions. It is a philosophical text, and its importance in Jainism is comparable with that of the Brahma Sutras and Yoga Sutras of Patanjali in Hinduism. In an aphoristic sutra style of ancient Indian texts, it presents the complete Jainism philosophy in 350 sutras over 10 chapters. The text has attracted numerous commentaries, translations and interpretations since the 5th-century.

The first verse of Tattvārthsūtra, “सम्यग्दर्शनज्ञानचारित्राणि मोक्षमार्ग:” summarizes the Jaina path to liberation. It means that the Ratnatraya (three jewels: right view, right knowledge and right conduct) collectively constitutes the path to liberation or moksha.
Of these three, सम्यग् दर्शन (samyag darśana), सम्यग् ज्ञान (samyag jñāna), and सम्यक् चारित्र (samyak cāritra), it is the cāritra or conduct that I’m examining here. The five kinds of spiritual purity in right conduct are given commentary in Arun Kumar Jain’s Faith and Philosophy of Jainism (p. 129-30):
The Minor Vows are: ahimsa, truth, nonstealing, celibacy, and nonpossession. They are called “Minor” (anuvrata) because the householder observes them in a modified way. In their full observance by monks, they are called Mahavratas.
Ahimsa is the foundation of Jain ethics. Bhagwan Mahavir called it pure, universal, everlasting. It says: ‘one should not injure, subjugate, enslave, torture or kill any animal, life form, organism or sentient being. This is the essence of religion. It embraces the welfare of all animals, visible and invisible. It is the basis of all stages of knowlege and the source of all rules of conduct. The Holy Scriptures analyze the divine and practical aspects of ahimsa and discuss the subject negatively and positively.’
and (p. 131):
In addition to the Five Minor Vows, the householder observes three Social Vows that govern his external conduct in the world. Then there are four Divine vows that reflect the purity of his heart. They govern his internal life and are expressed in a life that is marked by charity (dana).

In order to preserve the integrity of the principle vows listed above, the Jain thinkers have prescribed sub-vows as precautionary means. First, there is reference to the Salyas or disturbing factors such as ignorance, deceit, and self-interest, from which an individual should free himself. The salyas represent the negative requirements for the ideal practice of the vratas [vows]. Also, there are four Bhavanas (virtues) that represent the positive means of supporting the Vratas. These qualities, which a votary of ahimsa must possess, are maitri (love, friendship), pramoda (joy and respect), karunya (compassion), and madhyastha (tolerance toward life forms).

The Tattvārthsūtra (Chapter 7, sutra 11) lists these four bhavanas or right sentiments: मैत्री (maitrī), प्रमोद (pramoda), कारुण्य (kāruṇya), and माध्यस्थ (mādhyastha). As their language is affirmation, even references specifically as such, they for me are the proper focus of qualificatory development in my present study of Jainism. So this is how I’ll proceed.
